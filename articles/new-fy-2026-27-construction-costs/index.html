Home Loan Rate Outlook — FY 2026-27

The RBI's monetary policy trajectory for FY 2026-27 suggests potential for one more 25 bps repo rate cut in H1 2026-27 (likely August–October 2026), if inflation remains below the 4.5% threshold. Current repo rate: 6.25%. Impact on borrowers:

  • Each 25 bps RBI rate cut typically translates to 20–25 bps reduction in bank lending rates (lag of 1–3 months)
  • On a ₹50 lakh loan for 20 years, a 0.25% rate reduction saves ₹160/month in EMI and ₹38,000 over the loan tenure
  • If you're already paying a floating rate loan, no action needed — the benefit passes through automatically
  • If you're planning a new loan: floating rate is better than fixed rate given the expected downward rate trajectory

Action Plan: 10 Things to Do Before March 31, 2026

  1. Get 3 contractor quotes now — quote before April 1, contract now to lock in FY25-26 rates
  2. Finalise your floor plan — design changes after contract signing are expensive and delay-prone
  3. Get home loan pre-approval — pre-approval gives you negotiating power and locks your rate
  4. Check PMAY eligibility — if your income qualifies, apply before disbursement
  5. Buy cement and steel early — if you have space, pre-purchase 2–3 months' supply
  6. Order modular kitchen and wardrobes — factory lead times are 4–6 weeks; order in March, receive in April–May
  7. Lock in tile prices — visit dealers and get fixed-price invoices for your tile selection
  8. Finalise your interior designer / contractor — good contractors get booked 2–3 months ahead, especially in metros
  9. Check municipal approvals timeline — don't start until you have your building permit; delays post-start are far more expensive
  10. Build your contingency fund — target 15% of total project cost as cash reserve before starting

Bottom Line

  • FY 2026-27 construction costs will be 7–11% higher than FY 2025-26. A ₹25 lakh project started in April instead of March costs ₹1.75–2.75 lakh more.
  • Lock in contractor contracts and material prices before March 31 wherever possible. The financial year transition triggers price revisions across the supply chain.
  • Home loan rates may ease slightly in H2 2026-27 if RBI cuts the repo rate — but don't wait for rates to bottom out before buying.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much will construction costs increase in FY 2026-27?+

Construction costs are expected to increase 7–11% in FY 2026-27 compared to FY 2025-26, driven by higher cement prices (4–7%), TMT steel increases (5–8%), and continued labour wage inflation (8–12%). Metro cities will see higher increases due to tighter labour supply. Locking in contractor contracts before April 2026 is advisable.

Is April–June the best time to start construction in India?+

April and May are generally good months to start in North and Central India before peak monsoon arrives. For coastal cities (Mumbai, Chennai) and the South, construction can continue year-round with planning. Starting foundation work in March–April gives you the pre-monsoon period to complete structural work, then resume finishing post-monsoon in October.

What should I do before April 2026 to save on construction costs?+

Three high-impact actions: (1) Sign your contractor contract and lock in material prices before the FY 2026-27 price escalations kick in. (2) If you have storage, procure 3–4 months of cement and steel now. (3) Apply for home loan approval and PMAY subsidy before the April rate revision cycle at major banks.

Will home loan interest rates change in FY 2026-27?+

The RBI maintained the repo rate at 6.25% in early 2026 after cuts in 2025. Market expectations suggest one more 25 bps cut possible in H1 2026-27 if inflation remains benign. This would reduce floating rate home loans by ~0.25%, saving ₹150–300/month on a ₹50 lakh loan. New borrowers should opt for floating rate loans now.

How has construction cost changed over the last 3 years in India?+

Standard quality construction in metro cities has increased from approximately ₹1,550/sqft in FY 2023-24 to ₹1,750/sqft in FY 2024-25 and ₹1,900–2,000/sqft in FY 2025-26. This represents a 25–30% cumulative increase over 3 years, driven primarily by steel price volatility (2022–23 spike), cement consolidation, and labour wage increases post-COVID.
